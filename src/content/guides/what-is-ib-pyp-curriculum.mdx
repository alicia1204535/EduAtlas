## Why Parents Are Asking About the IB PYP

The IB Primary Years Programme (PYP) is the most widely offered primary curriculum across Singapore's international schools. Over 25 schools offer it here, from premium institutions like UWCSEA to mid-range schools like CIS and Nexus. If you're researching international primary schools in Singapore, you'll encounter the PYP constantly.

But what exactly is it? How does it differ from the British or American primary approaches? And is it right for your child?

---

## The Big Picture: What Makes PYP Different

### Traditional Primary Education
Subjects taught separately. Teacher delivers content. Students memorize and reproduce knowledge. Tests and exams measure learning.

### IB PYP
Subjects integrated around big ideas. Teacher facilitates inquiry. Students construct understanding through questions and projects. Portfolios and exhibitions demonstrate learning — no standardized tests.

The core philosophy: **children are natural inquirers**. The PYP harnesses this rather than suppressing it.

---

## The PYP Framework: Six Transdisciplinary Themes

Instead of organizing learning around subjects (Math, English, Science), the PYP organizes around six **transdisciplinary themes** that recur each year with increasing complexity:

| Theme | What It Explores |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Who We Are** | Identity, beliefs, health, relationships |
| **Where We Are in Place and Time** | History, geography, migration, discoveries |
| **How We Express Ourselves** | Creativity, culture, communication, art |
| **How the World Works** | Science, technology, natural phenomena |
| **How We Organize Ourselves** | Systems, communities, economies, governance |
| **Sharing the Planet** | Environment, sustainability, justice, peace |

Within each theme, students learn Mathematics, Language, Science, Social Studies, Arts, and Personal/Social/Physical Education — but these subjects are taught in context, not as isolated periods.

### Example: "How the World Works" in Grade 2

A unit on **weather** might involve:
- **Science**: Observing and recording weather patterns
- **Mathematics**: Graphing temperature data, calculating averages
- **Language**: Writing a weather report, reading nonfiction texts about climate
- **Arts**: Illustrating different weather conditions
- **Social Studies**: How weather affects communities around the world

The same skills as traditional subjects — but connected.

## Key Elements of the PYP

### Learner Profile: 10 Attributes

Every PYP school cultivates these attributes:

- Inquirers, Knowledgeable, Thinkers, Communicators, Principled
- Open-minded, Caring, Risk-takers, Balanced, Reflective

These aren't just buzzwords — they're integrated into daily classroom language, report cards, and parent conferences.

### Key Concepts: 7 Big Ideas

Form, Function, Causation, Change, Connection, Perspective, Responsibility.

These are the lenses through which students explore the transdisciplinary themes. Instead of asking "What is a government?" (form), students ask "How does government affect people's lives?" (connection/perspective).

### Approaches to Learning (ATL Skills)

Thinking skills, communication skills, social skills, self-management skills, and research skills — explicitly taught and practiced across all units.

### Action

PYP students are expected to take action based on their learning. This could be:
- A personal choice (using less water)
- A classroom initiative (starting a composting programme)
- A community project (raising funds for a cause)

The goal: learning that leads to real-world impact, not just test scores.

## How Assessment Works in the PYP

There are **no standardized tests** in the PYP. Assessment looks radically different from what most parents grew up with:

| Method | What It Looks Like |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Formative assessment** | Ongoing observation, feedback, self-reflection — happens daily |
| **Summative assessment** | End-of-unit projects, presentations, portfolios — demonstrates understanding |
| **Student-led conferences** | Your child presents their learning to you, with teacher facilitation |
| **The Exhibition (Grade 5/6)** | A major, student-led inquiry project — the PYP's capstone experience |
| **Written reports** | Narrative-based, describing progress against learning outcomes |

### What You Won't See
- Percentage scores
- Letter grades
- Class rankings
- "Your child is below average" comparisons

This can be disorienting for parents used to traditional report cards. The PYP values *growth over time* rather than *ranking against peers*.

## PYP vs British Primary vs American Elementary

| Dimension | IB PYP | British Primary | American Elementary |
|-----------|--------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Structure** | 6 transdisciplinary themes | Discrete subjects (English, Maths, Science) | Discrete subjects |
| **Teaching** | Inquiry-driven | Mix of direct instruction and activities | Varies by school |
| **Assessment** | No standardized tests | SATs (Year 2, Year 6), teacher assessment | MAP testing, teacher grading |
| **Curriculum** | IB framework — schools adapt | English National Curriculum (prescribed) | State standards (varies) |
| **Best for** | Children who learn by asking questions | Children who thrive with structure and clarity | Children targeting US education pathway |

## Is the PYP Right for Your Child?

### The PYP Is a Great Fit If Your Child...
- Asks "why?" constantly
- Learns best through hands-on exploration
- Enjoys working in groups
- Has diverse interests rather than a single focus
- May relocate between countries (PYP is offered at 2,200+ schools globally)

### You Might Prefer Another Approach If Your Child...
- Prefers clear, structured daily lessons
- Thrives on "right answer" feedback
- Needs explicit, sequential skill-building (especially in mathematics)
- Performs best with traditional testing and grading

## Common Parent Concerns (and Honest Answers)

**Q: Will my child fall behind if we return to a national curriculum?**
A: PYP students typically transition well — the key skill is *learning how to learn*. Math may require some adjustment if transferring to a curriculum with a different sequence (e.g., British or Singapore national). Most international schools offer bridging support.

**Q: How do I know my child is "on track" without grades or tests?**
A: PYP schools provide detailed narrative reports, regular parent-teacher conferences, and student portfolios. You'll have more insight into your child's learning — just in a different format. If you need standardized benchmarks, many PYP schools also administer external assessments like MAP (Measures of Academic Progress) to complement PYP assessment.

**Q: Is the PYP just "progressive education" with less academic rigor?**
A: No. The PYP has clear, rigorous learning outcomes. The difference is in *how* children reach them — through inquiry, not memorization. UWCSEA and other top PYP schools produce students who perform excellently on external benchmarks like IGCSEs and IB Diploma.

**Q: Can my child join a PYP school mid-programme?**
A: Yes. PYP schools are experienced with integrating new students. The inquiry approach means children are accustomed to collaborative learning with diverse classmates.

## How to Evaluate a PYP School

When visiting a PYP school, look for:

- **Evidence of inquiry** — Student questions displayed on walls, not just teacher-created materials
- **The classroom environment** — Flexible seating, resources accessible to children, learning in progress (not pristine displays)
- **Student voice** — Ask students what they're learning about; PYP students should be able to articulate their inquiries
- **The Exhibition** — If visiting a Grade 5/6 class, ask about the Exhibition — the quality of these projects reveals the school's PYP implementation
