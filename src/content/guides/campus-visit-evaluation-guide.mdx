## Why Campus Visits Matter More Than Websites

A school's website shows you its best self. A campus visit shows you its real self. The difference between a glossy brochure and actual classrooms — between marketing photos and how teachers interact with students — can be striking.

This guide gives you a structured approach to campus visits so you can compare schools objectively.

---

## Before You Visit: Preparation

### When to Visit
- **During a school day** — see real teaching and learning, not an empty campus
- **Not during school holidays** — check the school's academic calendar
- **Not during the first or last week of term** — chaotic times that don't reflect normal operations

### What to Bring
- This checklist (or your own)
- Notebook and pen
- Your child (if possible — their reaction matters)
- List of your top 3 priorities (curriculum, budget, location — or whatever matters most)

---

## Part 1: First Impressions Matter

### Arrival
- Was communication before the visit professional and timely?
- Are you greeted warmly at reception?
- Is there a clear visitor management system (security check, badge)?

**Red flag**: You're left waiting 15+ minutes without explanation. If they can't organize a scheduled tour, what does that say about daily operations?

### Campus Environment
- Is the campus clean and well-maintained without being sterile?
- Are there signs of student life — artwork on walls, projects displayed, student work visible?
- Do the facilities match the fees being charged?

**What to notice**: Schools should look lived-in and active, not like a furniture showroom.

---

## Part 2: The Classroom — Where It Matters Most

### During Classroom Walkthroughs

| What to Observe | What's Good | What's Concerning |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Student engagement** | Students asking questions, working in groups, actively participating | Silent rows of students copying from the board |
| **Teacher-student interaction** | Teachers at eye level, using student names, warm but professional | Teacher lecturing from the front, students disengaged |
| **Classroom walls** | Student work, inquiry questions, learning resources | Pristine walls with only teacher-made posters |
| **Student diversity** | Mixed nationalities, visible integration | Nationalities visibly segregated |
| **Technology use** | Integrated meaningfully into learning | iPads used as digital worksheets |

### Questions to Ask Students (If Possible)

- "What are you learning about at the moment?"
- "What's your favorite thing about this school?"
- "What would you change if you could?"

Listen to *how* they answer, not just what they say. Enthusiasm is genuine. Scripted answers are obvious.

---

## Part 3: 20 Questions to Ask

### Curriculum & Academics

1. **How do you support students who are ahead in some subjects and behind in others?**
2. **What does a typical school day look like for my child's grade?**
3. **How much homework should we expect?**
4. **How do you teach Mandarin (or our target language)? Is it daily? Differentiated by level?**

### Student Support

5. **What pastoral care and wellbeing support do you offer?**
6. **How do you handle bullying incidents?**
7. **What EAL (English as an Additional Language) support is available? At what additional cost?**
8. **Do you have learning support for students with mild additional needs (dyslexia, ADHD)?**

### The Teacher Factor

9. **What is the average teacher tenure at this school?** (Longer = better culture)
10. **How do you support teacher professional development?**
11. **Are teachers involved in after-school activities?** (Shows engagement beyond the classroom)

### Community & Culture

12. **How do you help new students transition? Is there a buddy system?**
13. **What does the parent community look like? Are there active parent groups?**
14. **How do you communicate with parents about student progress?**

### Practical Matters

15. **Is there a waitlist for my child's grade level? If so, what's realistic?**
16. **What are the exact additional costs beyond tuition?** (Get a written fee schedule)
17. **How does the school bus system work? What areas do you serve?**
18. **What after-school activities are included in fees vs. charged separately?**

### University & Beyond

19. **Where have your graduates gone to university in the last 3 years?** (Ask for a list, not percentages)
20. **What does university counseling look like? When does it start and how many counselors per student?**

## Part 4: Red Flags

### Serious Concerns
- Teachers appear stressed, unhappy, or avoid eye contact
- The tour guide only shows brand-new facilities and avoids older classrooms
- Student behavior is notably poor without adult intervention
- Questions are deflected with vague answers or "we're working on that"
- High teacher turnover is acknowledged but not explained

### Moderate Concerns
- The tour feels rushed (under 30 minutes)
- You don't see actual teaching — only empty classrooms
- The admissions officer doesn't ask about your child's interests or needs
- Fee information is vague or incomplete

## Part 5: After the Visit — Compare Objectively

Rate each school on a 5-point scale:

| Factor | Your Priority Weight | School A | School B | School C |
|--------|---------------------|---------|----------|----------|
| Curriculum fit | | | | |
| Teacher quality (observed) | | | | |
| Student engagement (observed) | | | | |
| Facilities | | | | |
| Location / commute | | | | |
| Cost (all-in) | | | | |
| Your child's reaction | | | | |
| Parent community | | | | |
| University outcomes | | | | |
| **Weighted Total** | | | | |

Multiply each score by your priority weight. The school with the highest weighted total is your objective best fit — though your gut feeling matters too.

---

## The One Question That Reveals Everything

At the end of the tour, ask:

> **"If you could change one thing about this school, what would it be?"**

A thoughtful, honest answer tells you the school is self-aware and continuously improving. A polished "nothing, we're perfect" answer is either dishonest or delusional — neither is good.
